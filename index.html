Conquer the Suffering: A Mixed-Reality Himalayan Soundscape

We are pleased to present Conquer the Suffering, a collaboration between Dr. Cecilia Wu and Daniel Schreck, for consideration under the Rubin x Art Projects Grant Program. This project aligns with the Rubin Museum’s vision of expanding into digital spaces, creating a "museum without walls" where Himalayan art and wisdom can reach audiences beyond physical boundaries. By merging traditional Buddhist ritual sounds with modern technology, Conquer the Suffering offers an immersive experience that preserves and reinterprets ancient traditions for contemporary engagement.

Dr. Wu’s expertise in music production, spatial audio, and sound design brings together Nepalese, Bhutanese, and Tibetan Buddhist ritual instruments, spiritual chants, and electronic composition to create deeply resonant surround sound environments. These Himalayan immersive soundscapes interact with Daniel Schreck’s virtual reality landscapes in real-time, designed to transmit Tibetan Buddhist wisdom through cross-platform spatial computing systems such as Unity, OpenXR, and RealityKit. Using real-time Ambisonics surround sound technology, the evolving soundscapes and visuals work together to create an engaging dynamic environment—whether unfolding as mandala-like fractal art structures, shifting gradients of light, or tranquil mountain vistas—all reinforcing themes of resilience, introspection, and transformation.

As the Rubin Museum expands its digital presence, this project offers a powerful and innovative way to engage global audiences in Himalayan contemplative practices through modern technology. By leveraging VR, Conquer the Suffering extends beyond traditional museum experiences, making these teachings more accessible, engaging, and interactive. This fusion of ancient wisdom and contemporary innovation supports the museum’s mission to preserve and share Himalayan culture in ways that resonate with today’s audiences. We believe this collaboration is not only well-aligned with the Rubin Museum’s evolving direction but also a valuable contribution to its future initiatives.

Technologies

Ambisonics

Ambisonics encodes a full 360-degree soundfield, capturing audio from every direction and maintaining spatial accuracy as the listener moves. This format integrates diverse inputs—vocals, instruments, and electronic elements—into a seamless sonic sphere. When synced with VR visuals, it heightens immersion and realism, allowing participants to experience nuanced shifts in perspective for a deeply meditative encounter.

Unity

Unity is a leading development platform for creating immersive graphical experiences that can be deployed across diverse headsets, including Oculus and Apple Vision Pro. Its flexible design tools, real-time rendering, and integrated VR libraries empower artists to craft dynamic virtual worlds, embedding interactive elements and rich lighting. This cross-platform reach ensures broad accessibility for transformative, visually engaging installations.

Virtual Reality

Virtual reality environments will support the presentation immersive experiences that respond dynamically to the composition’s soundscapes. By offering environments that are platform-agnostic and can run on headsets of the user's choice, Conquer the Suffering aims to transmit Himalayan wisdom culture through a multi-sensory journey that immerses the viewer into experiential landscapes of light and contemplation. The cross-platform reach of these environments supports broad accessibility to these wisdom spaces for years to come.

Audiovisual Foundations

Here, we present two works by Dr. Wu that form the conceptual and artistic foundations for the project vision.

Sound of Nangqên is an electroacoustic audiovisual composition that explores the interplay between sound, space, and culture, inspired by Tibet’s "Highland of Zen" in Nangqên County, Qinghai Province, China. Fully permitted, supported, and funded by the Nangqên County, the local monasteries, and the Tibetan people, this work immerses listeners in the region’s rich spiritual and cultural soundscape, integrating 20 gigabytes of field recordings—encompassing natural environments, local voices, folk songs, daily life, and traditional Tibetan instruments—with analog and digital electronic processing. We envision Sound of Nangqên as an audiovisual inspiration for the immersive environments that we will develop to accompany Conquer the Suffering. A potential avenue for artistic development is virtual environments for Sound of Nangqen itself, offering users the experience of spatial surround audio together with immersive Himalayan-inspired visuals.

Conquer the Suffering is a musical exploration that draws inspiration from Nepali diverse musical traditions, capturing its spiritual soundscapes, cultural nuances, and the people's resilience. This composition is constructed from the sounds of traditional Nepali and Tibetan Buddhist ritual instruments, soulful vocals, and nuanced electronic elements. The visual component of Conquer the Suffering will be the central focus of artistic development for the present project.

Potential Directions

We include the following set of potential environments to indicate the possible directions of artistic development. We envision Conquer the Suffering as a synthesis of several immersive virtual-reality environments with interwoven displays of light and sound.

A sunlit Tibetan plateau with rolling grasslands, white-walled monasteries, and fluttering prayer flags.

Shakyamuni Buddha glows with golden light, as shifting fractal patterns radiate from his luminous form.

Potential lines of development include environments that support interactivity with each of the Eight Auspicious Symbols.

VR environments offer a space to view—and, eventually, interact with—yidams (meditational dieties), such as 1000-armed Avalokiteśvara.

Contributors

Dr. Jiayue Cecilia Wu

Dr. Jiayue Cecilia Wu (武小慈) (CV) is a scholar, composer, audio engineer, multimedia artist, and vocalist whose work explores the intersection of music, technology, and healing. Originally from Beijing, she blends Eastern philosophy with cutting-edge innovation to create immersive sonic experiences. With a background in Design and Engineering, Dr. Wu spent a decade as a composer, producer, and publisher with EMI Records and Universal Music Group before pursuing advanced research in audio. She earned an M.A. in Music, Science, and Technology from Stanford University’s Center for Computer Research in Music and Acoustics (CCRMA) and a Ph.D. in Media Arts and Technology from UC Santa Barbara.

Since 2018, she has been an Assistant Professor and Graduate Program Director at the University of Colorado’s College of Arts and Media. Her multimedia compositions, installations, and films—over 100 in total—have been exhibited at prestigious venues, including the National Museum of China, Denver Art Museum, IEEE, Society for Electro-Acoustic Music in the United States (SEAMUS) , International Computer Music Conferences (ICMC), New Interfaces for Musical Expression (NIME), International Symposium on Electronic Art (ISEA), and international film festivals in Berlin, LA, London, New York, Seoul, and Toronto. A former U.S. National Academy of Sciences Sackler Research Fellow, she has received grants and awards from the Audio Engineering Society (AES), NAMM, College of Music Society, Stanford University, the State of Colorado, and the California State Assembly.

Dr. Wu is a leading advocate for inclusivity in music technology, serving as President of the Association for Technology in Music Instruction (ATMI), Board Governor and DEI Committee chair of AES, as well as Board Director-at-Large of the International Computer Music Association. Her book Sound from Within: Phenomenology and Practice (Springer Nature, 2021) explores the healing potential of sound. She regularly lectures at R1 universities and leads global Embodied Sonic Meditation workshops, redefining the relationship between sound, science, and human experience.

Daniel Schreck

Daniel Schreck (Resume) is a graduate student in the Computer Science department at Stanford University, exploring the intersection of AI, virtual reality, and contemplative practices. A M.S. candidate in the artificial intelligence track, he specializes in system design and HCI inspired by modern cognitive science. Originally from New York City, he earned his Sc.B. in Neuroscience from Brown University, where he conducted research in retinal neurobiology at the Berson Lab and the cognitive impacts of meditation in the CLAN-Lab, investigating the neural correlates of meditative states. Daniel's work spans machine learning, computer vision, and human-computer interaction, with a present focus on building immersive VR environments for meditation. His current projects include Silo, a neuroscience-driven application for reducing screen-time addiction, and YourDB, a cloud-based database infrastructure startup.

Passionate about education and mentorship, Daniel has served as a teaching assistant for CS107 (Computer Systems), guiding students through systems programming, memory management, and software engineering principles, and CS193p (iOS Development with SwiftUI), preparing the course for free access to the public. Today, he's inspired by findings on gamma-wave oscillation patterns in meditators within Tibetan traditions (e.g. Davidson et al.), and he is interested in developing VR technologies that help meditators access a variety of contemplative states and practices.
